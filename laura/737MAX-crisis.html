Section 1

Key vocabulary

Words and phrases from the video. Read through before watching, then revisit after.

angle of attack
noun phrase
The angle between the wing and the oncoming air. Too high an angle of attack can cause a stall.
"The angle of attack sensor was sending faulty data to the flight computer."
stall
noun / verb
When a wing loses lift because the angle of attack is too high. The aircraft stops flying — it doesn't stop moving. Not the same as an engine stall.
"MCAS was designed to prevent the aircraft from entering a stall."
horizontal stabiliser
noun
The small "wing" at the tail of the aircraft. It controls pitch — nose up or nose down. MCAS moved it automatically.
"The system moved the horizontal stabiliser to push the nose down."
to certify / certification
verb / noun
The official process where a regulator (like the FAA) confirms an aircraft is safe to fly. Without certification, no airline can operate the plane.
"Boeing wanted the MAX certified as a variant of the 737, not as a new aircraft."
to delegate
verb
To hand responsibility to someone else. The FAA delegated parts of the safety review to Boeing itself.
"The FAA delegated the evaluation of MCAS to Boeing's own engineers."
to ground (a fleet)
verb
To officially stop an aircraft type from flying. All 737 MAX aircraft were grounded worldwide after the second crash.
"Regulators around the world grounded the MAX fleet."
fuel-efficient
adjective
Using less fuel for the same journey. The A320neo's new engines were more fuel-efficient, which pressured Boeing to respond.
"Airlines wanted a more fuel-efficient narrow-body aircraft."
to cut corners
idiom
To do something the cheapest or fastest way, sacrificing quality or safety. Boeing cut corners on pilot training and system transparency.
"Critics say Boeing cut corners to get the MAX to market faster."

Section 4

Prepositions in aviation

The video uses spatial prepositions constantly. In aviation English, the wrong preposition can change the meaning completely.

Preposition pattern Meaning / context Example
under the wing position: engines are mounted under (= beneath) the wing "The engines hang under the wing on pylons."
further forward on repositioned toward the front of something "They had to mount the engines further forward on the wing."
at the back of at the rear; the stabiliser is at the back of the fuselage "The horizontal stabiliser sits at the back of the aircraft."
at a high angle of attack at = in a particular state or configuration "At a high angle of attack, the aircraft becomes unstable."
into a stall into = entering a dangerous state (motion toward) "MCAS was supposed to prevent the aircraft from going into a stall."
out of the sky out of = departing from (dramatic: falling from the sky) "Two planes fell out of the sky."
behind (the competition) trailing; in a weaker position than a rival "Boeing found itself behind Airbus in the narrow-body market."

Comprehension answers
Q1. Airbus launched the A320neo with new, fuel-efficient engines. Airlines were ordering it in large numbers. Boeing felt it couldn't afford the time (and risk) of designing an entirely new aircraft, so it chose to update the existing 737 platform instead.
Q2. The new engines were physically larger (wider diameter). They wouldn't fit under the wing in the original position without scraping the ground, so Boeing had to mount them further forward and slightly higher on the wing. This changed the aircraft's aerodynamic behaviour — at high angles of attack, the engine position created an additional nose-up pitching force.
Q3. Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System. It was a software system that automatically moved the horizontal stabiliser to push the nose down if the aircraft's angle of attack got too high, in order to prevent a stall and to make the MAX handle like the older 737.
Q4. A single angle-of-attack sensor. The aircraft had two sensors, but MCAS only read data from one. If that one sensor sent faulty data (as happened on both flights), MCAS would activate unnecessarily — and there was no cross-check to catch the error.
Q5. Simulator training is extremely expensive. Airlines — especially low-cost carriers — wanted their pilots to move directly from the older 737 to the MAX without extra time or cost. Some airline contracts reportedly included price penalties if simulator training became necessary. If the MAX were classified as a new type rather than a variant, it would require a separate type rating.
Q6. Both aircraft experienced repeated nose-down commands from MCAS shortly after take-off. The pilots pulled the nose back up, MCAS pushed it down again, and this cycle repeated — each time pushing the nose further down — until the aircraft hit the ground. The radar flight paths showed a distinctive "sawtooth" pattern of climbs and descents.
Q7. The FAA delegated key parts of the MCAS safety evaluation to Boeing itself. Boeing did not classify MCAS as a safety-critical system, so it received less scrutiny. This self-certification model meant the company responsible for building the system was also responsible for judging its safety — a clear conflict of interest.
Q8. Open discussion. Accept any well-argued position. Key distinction to draw out: an engineering mistake is a failure of competence (you tried and got it wrong); corporate negligence implies knowingly accepting risk, cutting corners, or hiding information from regulators and pilots. The evidence suggests Boeing knew about MCAS limitations and chose to minimise them.
