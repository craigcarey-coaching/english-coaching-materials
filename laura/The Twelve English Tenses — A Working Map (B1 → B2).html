The Twelve English Tenses— a system, not a list to memorise

English doesn't really have twelve tenses. It has three times and four ways of looking at them. Once you see the system, the names stop mattering.

01 · The Hook

Why does English have so many?

In French, one form often does the work of two or three English forms. Choosing the wrong English form sounds odd to a native — even when the grammar looks correct on paper. Here is the question that unlocks almost everything.

"When did you arrive?"  vs.  "Have you arrived?"

Both are about an arrival. Both are grammatically correct. They are not interchangeable.

The first asks about a finished moment in finished time. The second asks about now — are you here yet?

The difference isn't really about time. It's about relationship to now. Hold that idea — it's the secret behind every contrast in this document.

02 · The Map

Three times. Four aspects. Twelve combinations.

English doesn't just place an event in time (past / present / future). It also marks how the event sits inside that time — finished, in progress, finished-with-effects, or ongoing-with-duration. That second layer is called aspect, and it's what French speakers often miss.

Reading the four aspects:
• Simple — a fact, a complete event, a habit.
• Continuous — an action in progress, framed in a moment.
• Perfect — finished before another point in time, with consequences for that point.
• Perfect Continuous — duration of an action up to a point, often still going.
03 · The Three Hardest Contrasts at B1 → B2

Fix these three and the rest fall into place.

i.Past Simple vs. Present Perfect

The single most common B1→B2 mistake. Ask one question: does the moment of speaking still matter?

Past Simple
subject + verb-ed
"I worked in a bakery for two years."
Use when: the action is finished AND the time is finished. The past is closed. Often a specific time word: yesterday, in 2022, last week, when I was a student.
Present Perfect
have / has + past participle
"I have worked in bakeries since 2020."
Use when: the action connects to NOW — still relevant, still possible, or the time period is unfinished. Often: since, for, ever, never, already, just, yet, this week, today.
Time-words tell you which one yesterday last year in 2022 when I was… → Past Simple
since 2020 for ten years ever never just yet this morning(if it's still morning) → Present Perfect
Quick check · Practice 1

Past Simple or Present Perfect?

A friend asks about your work history. "I in three different bakeries since 2020."
Telling a colleague about your last job. "I there last year for six months."
Someone asks if you know Galway well. "Yes — I here for four years."
Discussing your morning. "I a coffee at 8 this morning."

ii.Past Simple vs. Past Perfect

When two things happened in the past, the one that happened first moves backwards a step. That's all the past perfect does.

Past Simple
verb-ed
"When I arrived, the meeting started."
Reads as: I arrived, then the meeting started — same sequence as the sentence.
Past Perfect
had + past participle
"When I arrived, the meeting had started."
Reads as: the meeting started before I arrived. I walked in late. The past perfect signals "earlier than the other past."
The mental picture Imagine two dots on a timeline. The dot further left (earlier) gets had + past participle. The dot further right (later, the "main" past) gets simple past. Same logic as French plus-que-parfait.

iii.Three Ways to Talk About the Future

English future is not one form — it's a choice between three, and the choice depends on how sure and how planned the future is.

will
decision now
"I'll help you with that."
A decision made at the moment of speaking. Predictions, offers, promises.
going to
prior intention or evidence
"I'm going to start my own business."
An intention already decided, OR a prediction based on visible evidence ("Look at those clouds — it's going to rain").
present continuous
fixed arrangement
"I'm meeting the accountant on Tuesday."
A fixed arrangement with another person — diary date, appointment, ticket booked.
The mental picture will = the decision happens as you speak. going to = the decision was made before you spoke (or evidence is in front of you). present continuous = the calendar already says so.

You've decided this already; you have a plan. "I open my own bakery next year."
It's in your diary; the time is fixed. "I the supplier at three on Thursday."

05 · How to Keep This in Your Head

The one question that fixes most mistakes

Before you choose a tense, pause for one second and ask yourself: "Is the time finished, or does it still touch now?"

Past Simple closes the door on the past. Present Perfect leaves it open.

Use this document like a reference, not a textbook. Read one section per week. Re-do the practice with different examples from your own life — what you did yesterday, what you've done since you moved to Galway, what you're doing on Saturday.

The grammar lives in the using, not the reading.
