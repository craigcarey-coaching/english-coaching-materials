How a flight gets
onto the schedule

The industry whose consequences a pilot lives with every day — slots, turnarounds, banks, recovery — and the English to talk about it.

Tap any underlined word for its plain-English meaning.

Every flight you have ever taken began as a line in a spreadsheet, months before departure. Long before a single passenger books a seat, a team of network planners decides where the airline will fly, how often, and with which aircraft. Their job is to fill the schedule in a way that is both useful to passengers and profitablemaking money rather than losing it for the airline — and those two goals do not always agree.

The first big decision is the shape of the networkall the routes an airline flies, seen together. Some airlines build around a hubone central airport most flights pass through — one or two large airports through which most flights pass. A passenger flying from a small city to another small city does not fly directly; instead they connect through the hub. This hub-and-spokeroutes arranged like a wheel, meeting at a central hub model lets an airline serve hundreds of city pairs with relatively few routes, because every spoke connects to every other spoke through the centre. Low-cost carriers often prefer the opposite approach, point-to-pointflying straight between two cities, with no central hub, flying directly between cities and skipping the hub altogether. It is simpler, but it only works on routes with enough demand to fill an aircraft on their own.

Wherever an airline flies, it has to find a slota booked time to take off or land at an airport — permission to take off or land at a given airport at a given time. At busy airports, slots are strictly limited and fiercely guarded; an airline may hold a valuable early-morning slot for decades and treat it as one of its most precious assets. No slot, no flight, however much demand there is.

Then there is the aircraft itself. An aircraft only earns money when it is in the air, so airlines plan each one's rotationthe chain of flights one aircraft flies across a day to keep it flying as much as possible. The enemy of high utilisationhow many hours a day the aircraft is actually flying is time spent on the ground, which is why the turnaroundthe short ground time between two flights matters so much: the minutes between an aircraft arriving at the gate and pushing back for its next flight. Cleaners, caterers, baggage handlers, refuellers and the incoming crew all work at once, often in well under an hour. A delayed turnaround does not just affect one flight; it ripples down the whole rotation.

At a hub, flights are deliberately grouped into connecting bankswaves of flights timed so passengers can change planes — waves of arrivals landing close together, followed by a wave of departures, so that connecting passengers have time to change aircraft without waiting for hours. Designing these banks is a puzzle: too tight and people miss their connections, too loose and aircraft sit idle.

The people are scheduled too. Crews cannot simply be assigned to aircraft; their pairingsthe set of flights one crew works before returning to base — the sequences of flights a crew works before returning to base — must respect strict limits on flying hours and rest. A schedule that looks perfect on paper can collapse if there is no legal crew available to operate it.

Finally, planners build in the expectation that things will go wrong. Weather, technical faults and air-traffic restrictions cause disruptionwhen the normal schedule is broken by problems, and when an airline's carefully balanced schedule unravels, recovery teams work to get aircraft, crews and passengers back into position with the least possible delay. The best schedule is not the one that works in perfect conditions — it is the one that bends without breaking when conditions are not.

Did it land?

Question 1

Why does a hub-and-spoke network let an airline serve so many city pairs with relatively few routes?

Model answer — teacher only
Because every "spoke" city connects to every other one through the central hub, rather than needing a direct route of its own. A handful of routes into the hub creates a very large number of possible journeys.
Question 2

The text says an early-morning slot can be one of an airline's "most precious assets." Why is a slot so valuable?

Model answer — teacher only
At busy airports the number of slots is strictly limited, so they are scarce. Without one you simply cannot operate the flight, no matter how many people want to travel — so a good slot is effectively the right to do business at that time.
Question 3

Why does a single delayed turnaround affect more than just one flight?

Model answer — teacher only
Because the same aircraft flies a chain of flights (its rotation) across the day. A late turnaround pushes the next departure back, which delays the one after that — the delay "ripples down" the rotation.
Question 4 · inference

What is the trade-off a planner faces when designing connecting banks?

Model answer — teacher only
If the bank is too tight, passengers don't have enough time and miss connections. If it is too loose, the timing is safe but aircraft sit idle on the ground, lowering utilisation. The planner has to balance reliable connections against efficient use of aircraft.
Question 5 · in your own words

The final line says the best schedule "bends without breaking." What does that mean for how airlines plan?

Sample answer — teacher only
It means they don't plan only for the ideal day. They build in margin and recovery plans so that when weather or faults disrupt things, the schedule can absorb the shock and recover, rather than collapsing entirely.
iiSix words, properly

Depth, not a word list

hub
"one or two large airports through which most flights pass"
Meaninga central point that many things pass through or connect to.
Familya hub airport · the hub of (an industry) · a transport hub
Registerneutral; used well beyond aviation ("a hub for start-ups").
Reuse: Name a city that is a hub — and say what it is a hub for.
slot
"permission to take off or land… at a given time"
Meaninga reserved place in a timetable or sequence.
Collocateshold / lose / be allocated a slot · a take-off slot · a time slot
Registereveryday too: "a slot in my diary", "a parking slot".
Reuse: Ask me for a slot for something — be specific about the time.
turnaround
"the minutes between an aircraft arriving… and pushing back"
Meaningthe short time (and work) needed to get something ready to go again.
Familyto turn an aircraft around · a tight / quick turnaround · turnaround time
Registerbusiness-wide: "a fast turnaround on the report."
Reuse: Describe what has to happen during a 30-minute turnaround.
rotation
"the chain of flights it operates across a day"
Meaninga recurring cycle or sequence that an aircraft (or person) works through.
Collocatesan aircraft / crew rotation · come back into rotation · on rotation
Watchin French la rotation also = a single round trip — don't over-map.
Reuse: Sketch one aircraft's rotation: where does it start, and where does it end the day?
utilisation
"the enemy of high utilisation is time spent on the ground"
Meaninghow fully and productively something is used.
Familyhigh / low utilisation · to utilise · aircraft utilisation (hours/day)
Registerformal/technical; in speech, "keeping the aircraft busy" is the plain version.
Reuse: Why is a low-cost airline so focused on high utilisation?
disruption
"weather, technical faults… cause disruption"
Meaninga serious interruption to the normal running of things.
Familyto disrupt · disrupted flights · major / widespread disruption
Oppositesmooth running · normal operations
Reuse: Describe a time travel disruption affected you — what was the cause?

False friends in the air

The scheduling world is full of words that look French but mean something else in English. These six are worth flagging now and folding into her own glossary.

correspondence
FR correspondance → a connection (connecting flight)
scale
FR escale → a stopover · a layover
exploit a line
FR exploiter une ligne → to operate a route
rentable
FR rentable → profitable · worth running
report a flight
FR reporter un vol → to postpone a flight
delay
FR un délai → a time-frame / deadline (a delay = un retard)

Should we add this route?

Your airline is thinking about adding one new route from Paris. Talk me through how you would decide whether it is worth it — what would you need to know, and what could go wrong?

Land at least five of: network · hub · point-to-point · slot · demand · turnaround · utilisation · profitable · disruption. Position her as the decision-maker, not the student.
Prompts to feed if she stalls — teacher only
Is there enough demand to fill the aircraft? Can we get a slot at both airports, and at a useful time? Does it fit our network — does it feed the hub or stand alone point-to-point? Which aircraft flies it, and does that fit its rotation without hurting utilisation? Is the route likely to be profitable, and how exposed is it to disruption (weather, seasonality)?
