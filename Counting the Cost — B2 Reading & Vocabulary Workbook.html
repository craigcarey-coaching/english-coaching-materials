Counting the Cost

A comprehension and vocabulary workbook built around a news feature on the economic effects of Brexit.
Source. The New York Times, 23 June 2026 Skills. Reading · Lexis · Speaking · Writing Time. ≈ 90 minutes
Before you start

01

Before you read

Warm up before opening the article. Speak first, in your own words.

A · Talk it through
Discuss with a partner or note a few ideas.
  1. When a country leaves a large trading bloc, what kinds of economic effects might appear? Predict two.
  2. Some economic costs are easy to measure (lost exports, for example) and some are not. Why might the hidden costs matter just as much?
  3. In your professional field, have you seen a change that looked small at first but added up over time? Describe it briefly.

02

Reading comprehension

Read the whole article once for the general picture, then go back for the detail.

A · True, False or Not Given
Decide whether each statement agrees with the article (True), contradicts it (False), or isn't mentioned (Not Given).
  1. Economists broadly believe the UK economy would be larger today if the country had stayed in the EU.
  2. Economists agree precisely on the size of Brexit's economic cost.
  3. The UK's trading rules changed immediately after the 2016 vote.
  4. The 2021 trade agreement placed high tariffs on most goods.
  5. Food and agricultural exports were among the sectors hit hardest.
  6. Overall immigration to the UK fell after Brexit, as some of its supporters had predicted.
  7. London lost its position as Europe's largest financial centre.
  8. Analysts expect the UK to rejoin the EU within the next two or three years.
Section 2A — answers
  1. True. The article reports broad agreement that the economy is roughly 4–6% smaller than it would otherwise have been.
  2. False. Estimates differ and economists dispute the methodology — one study suggests up to 8%, while the consensus is 4–6%.
  3. False. Rules did not fundamentally change until 2021, after the formal exit (end of January 2020) and an 11-month transition.
  4. False. Tariffs stayed mostly at zero; the new friction came from paperwork, border checks and regulations.
  5. True. Food and agricultural exports fell by nearly a third.
  6. False. Immigration did not fall; there was a large influx from non-EU countries instead.
  7. False. London kept its place as Europe's biggest hub, though it lost some specific business.
  8. False. Analysts don't expect major change soon, and the governing party has ruled out rejoining the single market or customs union. (Accept "Not Given" with discussion — the timeframe is implied rather than stated outright.)
B · Find the figure
Scan the article for the numbers. Write a short answer.
  1. By what range of percentages do most economists think the economy is smaller?
  2. According to one Stanford-led study, by how much may GDP have been reduced?
  3. By roughly how much did UK exports to the EU fall — and imports from it?
  4. How far did agriculture and food exports fall?
  5. How many new trade deals has the UK signed, and how many countries do they cover?
  6. What share of UK trade does Europe still account for?
Section 2B — answers
  1. 4 to 6 percent smaller.
  2. Up to 8 percent, accumulating gradually over time.
  3. Exports fell about 12%; imports fell about 16%.
  4. By nearly 30 percent (about a third).
  5. 39 deals covering 72 countries.
  6. More than 40 percent of UK trade.
C · Read between the lines
Answer in full sentences, in your own words.
  1. The writer says the 2016 warnings were "wrong, but only in their timing." What does that mean?
  2. Why is it difficult for economists to measure Brexit's exact impact?
  3. One expert describes London's loss of financial business as "like a slow puncture." What does the comparison suggest about how the change happened?
Section 2C — model answers
  1. The predicted damage did happen, but it built up slowly over years rather than arriving as a sudden shock right after the vote.
  2. Other major events — the pandemic, US tariffs, the wars in Ukraine and Iran — hit the economy at the same time, so it is hard to separate Brexit's effect from everything else.
  3. It suggests a slow, steady leak rather than a sudden collapse: the city is losing business gradually and continuously, not all at once. (Accept any answer capturing "gradual / slow / continuous".)

04

Speak & write

Now use the language. You know this territory — speak as the professional you are, not as a language learner.

A · Discuss
Choose two and talk for a minute on each. Try to use words from Section 3.
  1. The article calls the opportunity cost — "all the things that have not happened" — the most important cost of all. Do you agree that what didn't happen can matter more than measurable losses?
  2. From your own experience, how does a gradual loss (a "slow puncture") affect a business differently from a sudden shock?
  3. Are trade barriers ever worth the political benefits a country hopes to gain? Argue both sides.
  4. Nearly half of Britons now say Brexit is going worse than expected. How much should public opinion shape a major economic decision?
B · Write
Choose one. Aim for 120–150 words and use at least five words from this workbook.
  1. Opinion. Was Brexit primarily an economic decision or a political one? Give reasons.
  2. Summary for a colleague. In your own words, summarise the article's main economic findings for someone who hasn't read it. Paraphrase — don't copy sentences from the text.
Section 4B — what a strong answer shows
  1. Range of target vocabulary used accurately (not forced in).
  2. Genuine paraphrase in the summary task — own wording, not lifted phrases.
  3. A clear position in the opinion task, supported with at least one specific reason or figure from the article.
  4. Linking across sentences (however, as a result, while, by contrast) rather than a list of separate facts.
