Remind or Remember

Two verbs, one French shadow. Learn to feel which one a native reaches for — and never say “it remembers me” again.
01

The native instinct

≈ 5 min

Forget the dictionary for a second. Here’s how a native brain actually splits these two:

The one rule under everything
Remembering happens inside your own head. Reminding is something the outside world does to you.

You remember all by yourself — no one and nothing has to trigger it. But you get reminded: an alarm, a photo, a person, a smell nudges the memory back for you. So remind always has a doer on the outside and a person on the receiving end. Remember never needs one.

The two-second native test
Can you add “…all by myself, in my own head”? → remember.
Is something outside doing the nudging? → remind.

“I remember her name” — I hold it in my own head. “Her voice reminds me of my sister” — her voice, on the outside, does the nudging. The moment there’s an external trigger and a person being nudged, you’re in remind territory.

Why French speakers slip here

Se souvenir de and se rappeler both land on English remember. But rappeler à quelqu’un and faire penser à land on remind. French keeps the same root rappel- for both jobs, so English forces a choice French never makes — and the reflex is to reach for the wrong one.

02

Rule, structure & the edges

≈ 8 min

The clearest difference isn’t meaning — it’s who and what each verb needs around it. Learn the shapes and the errors mostly disappear.

remember

to hold or call something back in your own mind · no outside doer, no person-object
remember + noun — I remember the meeting.
remember + that… — I remember that we met in Lyon.
remember + to + verb — Remember to lock the door. (don’t forget — future)
remember + -ing — I remember locking the door. (a memory — past)
✗ never takes a person as the thing that “makes” you remember: remember me to buy milk

remind

to make someone remember · always transitive · always needs a person
remind + someone — The alarm reminds me.
remind + sb + to + verb — Remind me to call her.
remind + sb + of + noun — You remind me of my brother. (resemblance)
remind + sb + about + noun — Remind me about the invoice. (prompt to act)
remind + sb + that… — Remind him that it’s due Friday.

The one distinction inside remind worth owning: remind someone of = it resembles / evokes (“this street reminds me of Rome”). remind someone about / to = it prompts you to act (“remind me about the deadline”). French rappeler covers both — English splits them by preposition.

The four francophone traps — say these out loud
It remembers me of my childhood.ça me rappelle… → It reminds me of my childhood.
Remember me to buy milk.rappelle-moi de… → Remind me to buy milk.
I remind my first holiday.je me rappelle… → I remember my first holiday.
Can you remember me the time?tu peux me rappeler… → Can you remind me of the time?

The edges & exceptions:

  • “Remember me to your mother.” — a fixed, slightly old-fashioned idiom meaning pass on my greetings. It’s the one place remember + person is correct, so don’t let it confuse the rule — treat it as a frozen phrase.
  • reminder (noun) = the thing that prompts you (“a calendar reminder”). Don’t confuse with remembrance = the act of commemorating (“a day of remembrance”).
  • “Do you remember me?” is correct English (= do you have a memory of me?). If you mean do I look familiar / like someone else, that’s “Do I remind you of anyone?”
C1 stretch · register & nuance
Reaching for the verb is B2. Reaching for its register is C1.
  • Softened prompts: “May I remind you that…” / “Might I remind you…” carry polite pressure in professional English — a controlled way to correct or push back.
  • Impersonal reminding: “It bears reminding that…” / “a timely reminder that…” lets you flag a point without naming who forgot.
  • remember, upgraded: in careful speech, as I recall, if memory serves, and to the best of my recollection replace a flat “I remember” and hedge your certainty.
  • Literary remind of: “put someone in mind of” = a bookish synonym for remind of (“the harbour put her in mind of Marseille”).

Repair the francophone slips produce

Each sentence has one error a French speaker would make. Fix it in your head, then reveal the correction and the reason.

1 · “The music remembered me my wedding day.”
“The music reminded me of my wedding day.”
External trigger (music) + person (me) + resemblance → remind … of. “Remembered me” is the signature error.
2 · “Please remember me to send the invoice tonight.”
“Please remind me to send the invoice tonight.”
You’re asking to be prompted → remind me to. (“Remember me to…” only survives in the greetings idiom.)
3 · “I don’t remind his surname, sorry.”
“I don’t remember his surname, sorry.”
No outside nudge, no person being prompted — it’s recall in your own head → remember.
4 · C1 Rewrite firmly but politely: “You forgot again that the report is late.”
“May I remind you that the report is still outstanding.”
The C1 move is “May I remind you that…” — it corrects and applies pressure without naming the failure directly.
Teach it back the Feynman test

You only truly know it when you can explain it simply. Write your answers, then check against a model. No peeking first.

Explain to a beginner, in one sentence each: when do I use remember, and when do I use remind?

A clear model:

“Use remember when the memory is happening inside your own head, with nothing and nobody triggering it — I remember her name.”

“Use remind when something on the outside nudges a person’s memory — it always needs that person: the alarm reminds me; you remind me of my brother.”

Did your explanation capture:
  • remember = inside your own head, no trigger needed?
  • remind = an outside trigger acting on a person?
  • remind always needs a person-object?
Your own examples: write one true sentence with remember, and one with remind … of and one with remind … to.
Check each one:
  • remember: is there genuinely no external trigger and no person being nudged?
  • remind of: is it a resemblance/evocation (X is like Y)?
  • remind to: is it a prompt to act, with a clear person as object?
  • Did “remind” always keep its person-object? If any is missing, it’s wrong.

Teaching note

The single highest-value correction is killing “it remembers me (of)”. If a student produces it, don’t just supply “reminds” — make them state who is the trigger and who is being nudged. Naming the external doer fixes the reflex faster than the rule does.
