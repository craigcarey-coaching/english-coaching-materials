Phrasal Verbs

A verb plus a small word that quietly changes everything. Learn to see the particle — and to use it without thinking.

One verb. Many meanings.

A phrasal verb is a verb plus a particle — a small word like up, out, into, off. The particle is not pointing anywhere. It is part of the meaning. Change the particle and you change the whole word.

Watch one verb shift as the particle changes:

look upfind information
look intoinvestigate
look aftertake care of
look forward tofeel excited about

Same verb. Four unrelated meanings. The work is done by the particle.

Don't translate the particle. The meaning lives in the combination, not in the separate words. give up has nothing to do with "give" or "up". Learn the pair as a single new word — and absorb it from examples, not from a dictionary of parts.

Some you can guess. Some you simply have to know.

Four things to keep in your head

Not grammar to recite — habits to build. The fourth one is where most mistakes happen.

1

The particle carries the meaning

Treat verb + particle as one unit. Don't read the words separately. put off = postpone; put up with = tolerate. Different particle, different word.

2

Separable vs inseparable separable inseparable

Some phrasal verbs let the object sit in the middle; some don't.

I turned off the light  /  I turned the light off  separable
She looks after the children  inseparable
She looks the children after
3

The pronoun rule high-value

With a separable phrasal verb, a pronoun object (it, them, him, her) must go in the middle. This is the error to watch for.

Turn it off  ·  I'll bring it up  ·  Switch them on
Turn off it  ·  I'll bring up it  ·  Switch on them
4

Three-part verbs stay together

Verbs with two particles (come up with, get on with, look forward to) are always inseparable. The object comes after the whole chunk.

She came up with a plan  ·  She came up with it
She came a plan up with
