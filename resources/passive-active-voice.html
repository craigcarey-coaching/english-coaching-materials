A voice clinic · B2 · recognise it, then choose it

Active or Passive

Voice doesn’t change what happened — it changes who stands in the spotlight. Learn to hear the switch, then use it on purpose.
01

The native instinct

≈ 5 min

Same event, two camera angles. Watch what moves to the front:

Active
The engineer fixed the bug.
Passive
The bug was fixed (by the engineer).

Nothing about the event changed — the engineer still fixed the bug. What changed is who leads the sentence. In the active, the doer goes first. In the passive, the thing done to goes first, and the doer either follows with by… or vanishes completely.

The recognition fingerprint
Every passive carries the same DNA: a form of be + a past participle. was fixed · is being built · has been sent · must be signed.

The two-second test: find the main verb, then ask — is the subject doing it, or having it done to them? If you can naturally add “…by someone” to the end, you’re looking at a passive.

Why French speakers under-use it

French usually dodges the passive. It reaches for on — “on dit que…” → it is said that… / people say… — or a reflexive — “ça se vend bien” → it sells well. So francophones often keep everything active where careful English would go passive, and sometimes build the passive wrongly (“on says”). English also has a form French can’t mirror cleanly: the get-passive — “I got promoted.”

02

Rule, structure & the edges

≈ 9 min

The recipe never changes: subject + be (in the right tense) + past participle (+ by + doer). Only the form of be moves with the tense.

TenseActivePassive (be + past participle)
Present simpleThey clean the office.The office is cleaned.
Present continuousThey are cleaning it.It is being cleaned.
Past simpleThey cleaned it.It was cleaned.
Present perfectThey have cleaned it.It has been cleaned.
Past perfectThey had cleaned it.It had been cleaned.
Future / modalThey will / must clean it.It will be / must be cleaned.

The gate that stops most errors: only transitive verbs — ones that take an object — can go passive. “She arrived” has no object, so there is no passive of it (it was arrived). If nothing is being done to something, there’s no passive to build.

Why a native reaches for the passive — recognition matters because you’ll want to choose it on purpose:

The doer is unknown or irrelevant

My bike was stolen. (We don’t know who.)

The receiver is the real focus

The bridge was built in 1890. (The bridge is the point.)

Formal, scientific, procedural tone

The sample was heated to 200°C.

Tact — no one gets named

Mistakes were made. (Blame quietly disappears.)

Active is the default and usually the stronger choice. The skill is spotting the passive so you can decide — not sliding into it by accident, and not avoiding it where English expects it.

The francophone traps — say these out loud
It was happened last week.intransitive → no passive→It happened last week.
On says the meeting is cancelled.on dit que…→It is said (that)… / They say…
The report was write yesterday.wrong participle→The report was written yesterday.
This product sells itself very well.se vendre→This product sells very well.

The edges & exceptions:

  • State, not action. “The door was closed” can be a passive action (someone closed it) or just a description of a state. Add a doer to tell: “The door was closed by the guard” is clearly passive; “The door was closed and locked” is describing how it looked.
  • The get-passive. Informal English swaps be for get, often for sudden or unwelcome events: “He got fired,” “She got hurt.” Recognisable, but keep it for speech, not reports.
  • Verbs that resist the passive. Some transitive verbs of state won’t passivise: have, resemble, lack, cost, suit, weigh. “The car cost €20,000” has no passive (€20,000 was cost).
C1 stretch · the passives that hide
Spotting was fixed is B2. Spotting the passives in disguise is C1.
  • Middle voice — active in form, passive in meaning: “This book reads easily,” “The paint dries fast,” “The car won’t start.” No be + participle, yet the subject isn’t really doing anything.
  • Modal-perfect passives — three layers deep: “The email should have been sent yesterday,” “It could have been avoided.” Watch the stack: modal + have + been + participle.
  • Reporting passives — the formal way to distance a claim: “He is believed to have left the country,” “It is thought that talks will resume.”
  • Causative have / get something done — you arrange it, someone else acts: “I had my hair cut,” “We got the roof repaired.” Not a true passive, but easily confused with one — the subject causes the action rather than receiving it.

Judge & repair produce

Now the harder skill: is the passive right here, and is it built right? Fix each, then reveal.

1 · “The new policy was decided last month.” — Is this a good use of the passive? Why?
Yes — a strong, natural passive. Who decided it isn’t the point; the policy is. Naming a doer (“management decided the policy”) would only add clutter. This is exactly what the passive is for: keeping the focus on the receiver.
2 · Fix the form: “Three languages are speak in this office.”
“Three languages are spoken in this office.”
The participle of speak is spoken, not speak. The recipe is be + past participle — always the third form.
3 · Rewrite the French habit: “On has informed the staff about the change.”
“The staff have been informed about the change.”
On doesn’t survive into English. A hidden doer + focus on the receiver → present perfect passive: have been informed.
4 · C1 Spot the disguised passive and explain it: “The report should have been circulated before the vote.”
A modal-perfect passive stacked four deep: should (modal) + have + been + circulated (participle). It says the circulating didn’t happen but ought to have — regret + hidden doer in one move.
Teach it back the Feynman test

Explain it as if to a beginner, then check yourself. Teaching it is the proof.

In your own words: what is the difference between active and passive voice, and what is the one clue that tells you a sentence is passive?

“Active and passive describe the same event — the difference is who leads the sentence. Active puts the doer first (‘The team built it’); passive puts the thing done to first and pushes the doer away or drops it (‘It was built’).”

“The clue is the fingerprint: a form of be + a past participle — and you can usually add ‘…by someone’ to the end.”

Did you capture:
  • same event, different focus (not different meaning)?
  • passive = the receiver leads, doer hidden or after by?
  • the clue: be + past participle (+ optional “by someone”)?
Make the choice explicit: write one sentence where the passive is the better choice than the active — then say in a few words why.
A good answer shows one of these reasons:
  • the doer is unknown (My laptop was stolen),
  • the doer is obvious or irrelevant (The bins are emptied on Fridays),
  • the receiver is the real focus (The vaccine was developed in record time),
  • tact / formality (An error was made in the figures).
And check the form is clean: be + past participle, correct tense.

Teaching notes

Two failure modes to watch, and they’re opposite. (1) Over-avoidance: francophones keep everything active because French does; push them to use the passive in formal writing where English expects it (procedures, reports, news). (2) Mis-formation: the participle collapses to the base form (“was write / are speak”). Drill the third column of irregular verbs as a fixed reflex. The intransitive gate (“it was happened / it was arrived”) is worth a quick standalone check — if a verb can’t take an object, it can’t go passive, full stop.
