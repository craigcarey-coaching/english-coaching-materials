Chernow writes in a high, ornate register. Find each line in the passage, read it as written, then say it simply — the way you'd explain it to a friend over coffee. Say yours out loud first; only then lift the bar to compare. There's no single right answer — the bar just shows one plain version.

Target 1

He “feigned ignorance” and “disclaimed responsibility.”

Say it simply. What did Rockefeller actually do here?

He pretended he didn't know, and said it wasn't his fault.

Why it sounds elevated: two Latinate verbs (feign, disclaim) doing the work of plain ones (fake, deny). Swapping Latinate → Anglo-Saxon is the fastest way down the register ladder.

Target 2

An “inveterate gambler” who went straight from board meetings to his bookie's office.

Say it simply. Describe this man in everyday words.

He gambled non-stop — he'd walk out of a meeting and go straight to place a bet.

Notice: inveterate = a habit so old it's fixed. Plain swaps: hardened, lifelong, can't-stop.

Target 3

Rockefeller referred euphemistically to Carley's “want of balance.”

Say it simply. What was he really talking about — and what's he doing by phrasing it that way?

He used a polite phrase to avoid saying out loud that Carley had a gambling problem.

Register insight: want of is a formal way to say lack of. And a euphemism is exactly this — a soft phrase standing in for a blunt truth. A whole register move in two words.

Target 4

The firm had become “a byword for vicious tactics.”

Say it simply. What does it mean to be a byword for something?

When people wanted an example of brutal business, this was the name they reached for.

Useful frame: “X is a byword for Y” = X is so known for Y it stands as the example. Try building one about your own field.

Target 5

These were “anticompetitive practices” that “shaped future antitrust legislation.”

Say it simply. What's the cause-and-effect being claimed?

These were unfair tricks — and later laws were written specifically to ban them.

Your turf: this is the sentence where a biography of an oilman quietly becomes a story about your profession. Worth a pause.

Target 6

“Notwithstanding the public uproar,” Rockefeller claimed to be unaware.

Say it simply. Then: what plainer words could replace notwithstanding?

Even though people were angry in public, Rockefeller said he knew nothing about it. — Notwithstanding ≈ despite / even though / in spite of.

Callback: notwithstanding is a fronted concession — same job as the although / whereas connectors we worked on. Just a dressier suit.

Three quiet grammar notes (optional)

Causative have. Carley “had an underling dash off a letter.” The pattern have + person + base verb means you make/arrange for someone else to do it. Compare: I had my assistant send it.

Want of = lack of. An old, formal construction. For want of a better word… still survives in careful speech.

Participial openers. “No bluffer, Carley set up a store…” The sentence front-loads a verdict before the subject even arrives. It's a hallmark of this elevated style — and a tool you can borrow when you want to sound assured.
