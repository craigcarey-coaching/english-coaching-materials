01

The native instinct

≈ 5 min

A native speaker never “learns the rule” for these. They just hear which question the sentence is answering — and each word answers a different one.

for
How long?
for three hours · for years
a length of time
since
Starting when?
since Monday · since I arrived
the point you count from
during
When / inside what?
during the flight · during summer
inside a named period
over
Across what stretch?
over the weekend · over the years
spread through a span, often with change
ago
How far back?
two years ago · a moment ago
counts backwards from now
by
No later than when?
by Friday · by 5 p.m.
a deadline you must not cross
Picture the shapes
for is a stick you measure. since plants a flag and runs a line to now. during sits inside a box. over sweeps across it. ago points backwards. by is a fence.
Why French speakers slip here

French collapses distinctions English keeps:

depuis = since and for · pendant = for and during · il y a = ago · d’ici = by. So “depuis trois heures” pulls you toward since three hours when English wants for. Half the battle is just deciding which half of depuis you mean.

02

Rule, structure & the edges

≈ 9 min

Each word takes a particular kind of thing after it — and some pull a particular tense with them. Nail the pattern, not just the meaning.

WordTakesWatch forExample
fora length of timeany tenseWe talked for two hours.
sincea start pointpulls the perfect tenseI’ve worked here since 2020.
duringa named period / event (a noun)never + a lengthShe called during the meeting.
overa span you crossoften signals change / repetitionSales rose over the quarter.
agoa length + agopulls the past simpleI saw her three days ago.
bya deadline point= at or before; ≠ untilSend it by Friday.

The two splits that do most of the work:

  • since vs for — since needs a starting point (a clock or calendar moment: since 9 a.m., since Tuesday, since I moved); for needs a length (for 20 minutes, for a decade). When depuis appears, ask: point or length?
  • during vs for — during answers when? and takes a noun for a period (during the war); for answers how long? and takes a length (for six years). You can’t say during six years.
The five francophone traps — say these out loud
I’ve been waiting since twenty minutes.depuis vingt minutes→…waiting for twenty minutes.
We stayed during three hours.pendant trois heures→We stayed for three hours.
I have seen him two days ago.passé composé + il y a→I saw him two days ago.
I work here since 2019.présent + depuis→I’ve worked here since 2019.
I’ll finish it until Friday.jusqu’à ≠ d’ici→I’ll finish it by Friday.

The edges & exceptions:

  • during vs over — both sit inside a period, but over stresses the whole stretch: “I read during the flight” = at some point in it; “I read over the flight” = across the whole of it. Change and totals love over.
  • for can quietly disappear: “I’ve been here (for) three hours.” Dropping it is natural; adding since instead is not.
  • since = because too: “Since you asked, I’ll tell you.” Same word, different job — nothing to do with time.
  • in for a future length: “I’ll be back in an hour” (French dans). Not within an hour unless you mean the deadline sense.
C1 stretch · register & sequencing
B2 picks the right word. C1 controls the tense sequence and the formal register.
  • as of / as from — a formal start point: “The policy applies as of Monday.” Cleaner than “from” in contracts and notices.
  • by the time + clause forces a perfect: “By the time we arrived, the meeting had ended.” Sequence the two past events.
  • throughout / across stretch over further: “throughout the crisis” (every part of it), “across three decades” (a measured span).
  • negative + for: “I haven’t seen her for years” — the present perfect + for pairing that natives use to mean “and I still haven’t.”
  • fronted inversion: “Not since 2008 had markets fallen so sharply.” A high-register way to open with the start point.

Repair the francophone slips produce

One error per line, each a real French-speaker habit. Correct it, then reveal the fix and the reason.

1 · “I’m learning English since three years.”
“I’ve been learning English for three years.”
“Three years” is a length → for; and depuis here needs a perfect tense (I’ve been learning), not the present.
2 · “We discussed it during two hours.”
“We discussed it for two hours.”
A length can never follow during. during takes a named period; a measured length takes for.
3 · “I have visited Paris a month ago.”
“I visited Paris a month ago.”
ago fixes the event in the past → past simple, never the present perfect.
4 · C1 Sequence the tenses: “When we arrived, the train already left.”
“By the time we arrived, the train had already left.”
By the time + past pulls the earlier event into the past perfect (had left) to show it happened first.
Teach it back the Feynman test

Explain it plainly, then check yourself. If you can teach it, you own it.

In one sentence each, tell a beginner the difference between for, since and during.

“Use for when you say how long — a length: for two hours.”

“Use since when you name the start point you count from, and pair it with a perfect tense: I’ve worked here since May.”

“Use during when you name the period or event something happens inside: during the meeting.”

Did you capture:
  • for = a length (how long)?
  • since = a start point (+ perfect tense)?
  • during = a named period, never a length?

Teaching note

Every error on this page traces back to one of three French words: depuis (since/for), pendant (for/during), il y a (ago + tense). When a student slips, name the French word behind the slip before giving the English fix — it’s faster than re-teaching the whole grid. The since → perfect tense link is the one students under-notice: drill “I’ve worked / I’ve lived / I’ve known … since …” as a fixed frame.
